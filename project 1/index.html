Little Weirds

By Jenny Slate

Introduction to the Book

Little Weirds by Jenny Slate is a whimsical, deeply personal collection of essays that blends memoir, poetry, and surreal musings. Through fragmented yet richly detailed vignettes, Slate explores themes of love, heartbreak, self-discovery, and the magic of everyday moments. She writes with a dreamlike, often absurdist perspective, turning mundane experiences - like eating a croissant or taking a walk - into profound reflections on identity and belonging. Her lyrical prose and vivid imagery paint a world where emotions take physical form, and the boundaries between reality and imagination blur.

At its core, the book is about resilience and self-acceptance. Slate candidly discusses her struggles with anxiety, loneliness, and a painful divorce, but she approaches these hardships with a tender, almost childlike curiosity rather than bitterness. She finds beauty in vulnerability and humor in sorrow, crafting a narrative that feels intimate and comforting. Little Weirds is less about linear storytelling and more about capturing fleeting emotions, making it a heartfelt and unique meditation on what it means to be fully alive.

About the Author

Jenny Slate is a multi-talented writer, comedian, and actress known for her whimsical, poetic voice and introspective storytelling. As an author, she blends humor, surrealism, and raw emotion to explore themes of identity, love, heartbreak, and self-acceptance. Her writing, much like her comedic style, is both deeply personal and playfully absurd, often turning ordinary experiences into profound reflections. Some Goodreads Reviews

Marie on January 1, 2020

While reading this, I felt like I was having competing experiences: it was both surreal and heartbreakingly, achingly real. It was magical realism and non-fiction. It was at times so bright and hopeful and at times all of my saddest thoughts on paper. Slate has managed to capture her thoughts and emotions with a bit of magic and self-indulgence and commit them to paper. I saw one middling review call this book A LOT and it is! It is densely packed vulnerability, rapid fire thoughts and observations, and a certain taking up space that I admire. I've often made myself less, in a way Slate describes in this very book, to avoid that particularly review of my being. She's a lot. She's exhausting. Slate pours it all out unapologetically.
